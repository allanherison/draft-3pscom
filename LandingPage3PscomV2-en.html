3PSCOM - Polarization, Populism and Post-Truth

Who We Are

We are an international network of researchers from more than 20 countries that study social and political debates in social media with big data techniques and social network analysis.

Our Aim

We analyze three structural phenomena in democratic societies: Polarization, Populism and Post-truth (the three P's). Specifically, we focus on digital public debate processes during electoral campaigns and social discussions.

  • Define digital polarization, online populism and post-truth in theoretical terms.
  • Develop Social Network Analysis tools to measure the three phenomena.
  • Analyze these processes in case studies across America, Europe, Asia, and Oceania.

Organizer of the invited session "Big Data and Social Network Analysis for the 3 P's Processes: Polarization, Populism, and Post-Truth" at the 5th ISA Forum, Rabat (Morocco), 2025.

Big data and Social Network Analysis have become common in the social sciences. These methods are considered key to analyzing political communication in the digital public sphere, which is a space for citizens to express themselves on issues that affect the community. At a global level, the main recent crises such as Brexit in the UK, the COVID-19 pandemic, the invasions of Ukraine and Palestine, or the migration crises, among others, have generated an increase in societal polarization, populist discourse, and post-truth phenomena. This session is intended to receive proposals in which this type of big data and SNA analysis (such as Facebook, X, TikTok, or Instagram) are applied to different social and political processes. Specifically, processes of polarization, populist speeches that can promote hatred against certain groups and institutions, and the various forms of post-truth used by participants in digital debates. We aim to promote interdisciplinary collaboration in data science research to improve our understanding of phenomena such as debates during electoral campaigns around the world, public discussion processes on controversial issues, emerging social processes, social coordination, etc. The selected communications can shed light on the communication strategies used in the digital space and the different ways to neutralize the impact of these discourses on citizens' decisions when consuming information on social networks and contributing to its dissemination, especially when it comes to uncivil discourses, post-truth, or populism based on emotional and unreasoned arguments.

June 25-28, 2024

Faculty of Political Science and Sociology, UCM

The second meeting of the "3P's International Network" took place from June 25 to 28, 2024, at the Complutense University of Madrid (UCM). This session was part of several public workshops held at the Faculty of Statistical Studies and included a symposium at the Spanish Federation of Sociology Congress.

The "3P's International Network" is formed by researchers from 21 countries focusing on political Polarization, Populism, and Post-truth phenomena observed within digital social media debates. The network employs Big Data techniques and Social Network Analysis to address these phenomena comprehensively.

During the session held on June 26 at the Faculty of Political Science and Sociology, researchers engaged in an open debate, discussing and clarifying conceptual frameworks and methodological approaches concerning the three phenomena: Polarization, Populism, and Post-truth.

Interview with Jos√© Manuel Robles:

Jos√© Manuel Robles, co-director of the UCM Data Science and Soft Computing for Social Analytics and Decision Aids group, explained that the network focuses on digital interactions rather than simply counting messages. The team analyzes interactions like retweets and mentions, providing insights into populist or post-truth discourses' nature and dynamics.

He also highlighted differences in how social media is used across countries, noting that Twitter (X) is prevalent in Spain, Facebook in Eastern European countries, and TikTok in Brazil. The study concentrates on platforms like Instagram, Facebook, X, YouTube, and TikTok, depending on regional relevance.

Robles emphasized that although polarization is a significant concern, its intensity varies by topic and context, being stronger in spontaneous debates driven by breaking news compared to structured debates such as climate change discussions.

Populism has adapted significantly to social media, allowing populist leaders to engage directly and effectively with citizens, utilizing a simplified "us vs. them" rhetoric. Post-truth, another focus area, was described as manipulating facts to support particular narratives, a practice increasingly prevalent across all political spectrums.

Regarding spontaneity and intentionality in online debates, Robles pointed out the complexity of both organic and manipulated interactions, exacerbated by the intervention of artificial intelligence, often steering conversations toward greater polarization.

Objectives of the Network Meetings:

The primary aim of these gatherings is to share research progress, discuss new methodologies, and disseminate findings publicly. The event concluded with participation at the Spanish Sociology Congress in Seville, emphasizing the role of academic research in informing societal understanding and decision-making regarding polarization, populism, and post-truth.
